# MYS VOD Scripting Skill

VOD scripts for the 90-Day Account Plan have a completely different job from
YouTube scripts. YouTube earns attention. The VOD delivers on the promise that
attention made. The buyer has already paid. They don't need to be hooked —
they need to be guided. Clearly, practically, and without wasting their time.

---

## The Four-Part Structure — Overview

The course is built in four parts, in a fixed order. Each part depends on
the previous one. Do not move content between parts without good reason,
and flag any structural change for discussion before writing.

| Part | Topic | Core Job |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Territory Management | Establish real selling capacity. Calculate account floor. |
| 2 | Account Planning | Match planning depth to account tier. Structure the visit plan. |
| 3 | Call Management | Run every customer meeting with preparation, objective, and follow-up. |
| 4 | Opportunity Management | Apply the Five Filters. Build an honest pipeline. |

## The Worked Example — Sarah Mitchell

Sarah Mitchell is the fictional Area Manager used throughout all four parts.
Her details are fixed. Do not change them.

- **Role:** Area Manager
- **Sector:** Commercial cleaning supplies
- **Customer type:** Small businesses — hospitality, offices, light industrial
- **Accounts:** 170
- **Territory value:** £1.85 million
- **A accounts:** 43 (above the 80% line at £1.48M)
- **B accounts:** 34 (one visit per 8-week cycle)
- **Cycle slots:** 120 available (128 minus 8 reserved for prospecting)
- **Prospect list:** 10 active at any time from a long list

Sarah is used to make abstract calculations concrete and to give the buyer
something they can follow before applying the same process to their own numbers.
She's not a hero or a character — she's a calculation vehicle and a mirror.

When introducing Sarah in a new module, don't re-introduce her from scratch.
Reference the prior context briefly and move on. She's already been established.

## Writing Rules (Non-Negotiable)

**Full sentences at all times.** No fragments used as stylistic devices.
Each sentence does one job. VOD scripts are read from a teleprompter or
delivered to camera — every sentence needs to work when spoken aloud.

**Contractions throughout.** "Can't" not "cannot". "Don't" not "do not".
"It's" not "it is". "Haven't" not "have not". "Aren't" not "are not".
"Won't" not "will not". "Didn't" not "did not". The only exception is when
the emphasis specifically requires the full form.

**No YouTube energy.** No hooks, no pattern interrupts, no rhetorical
build-ups designed to earn attention. The buyer is already here. Speak
to them like a practitioner briefing a peer, not a presenter performing
for a camera.

**Methodical, not motivational.** The VOD is instructional. Its job is
clarity and application, not inspiration. Mark's authority comes from the
precision of the framework, not from enthusiasm about it.

## Section Structure

Each VOD part follows a consistent internal structure.

### Opening

Establishes the context for this part within the programme. Connects back
to where the buyer has come from (if Parts 2, 3, or 4) and sets up what
this part will cover. Does not repeat what's already been covered in detail —
a brief orientation is enough.

For Part 1, the opening also introduces Mark briefly and establishes the
programme's intent. This is the only place in the VOD where Mark's
background is introduced — don't repeat it in later parts.

**Opening length:** 150–250 words. Enough to orient, not enough to delay.

### Numbered Sections

Each concept gets its own section. Sections are numbered sequentially
within each part. The title of each section is plain and descriptive —
it tells the buyer exactly what this section is about. No clever titles.

**Section structure:**
1. **The concept stated plainly.** What this section is about, in one
   or two sentences. Not a hook — a clear statement.
2. **The logic explained.** Why this matters, how it works, what the
   principle is. Precise and unhurried.
3. **Sarah's numbers applied.** Walk Sarah through the calculation or
   decision. Show the output. State what it means for her territory.
4. **The buyer's application.** Direct the buyer to apply the same
   process to their own numbers. Reference the relevant supplemental
   download where applicable.
5. **The uncomfortable implication (where relevant).** If this section
   surfaces something the buyer might resist — accounts that don't
   belong where they think they do, time going to the wrong places —
   name it directly. Don't soften it. The framework is honest and
   the script should be too.

### Close

Ties the part together. States clearly what the buyer now has that they
didn't have at the start of this part. Sets up the homework or next step.
For Parts 1–3, sets up the next part briefly. For Part 4, closes the
programme.

**Close length:** 200–350 words. Enough to land properly.

## Tone Reference — Key Phrases from Locked Scripts

These are examples of the correct register. Study the pattern.

**On introducing a concept:**
"Before you can make any decisions about your accounts, you need to know
one thing..." — direct, no preamble, tells the buyer exactly where they're going.

**On the uncomfortable implication:**
"Here's what that means in practice. The other 127 accounts — nearly three
quarters of her list — are generating the remaining 20% of her revenue
between them." — states the data, lets it land, doesn't dramatise it.

**On addressing resistance:**
"The uncomfortable part of this exercise — and there always is one — is
the accounts that didn't make the cut." — names the resistance before
the buyer can voice it internally, then addresses it directly.

**On practical instruction:**
"Don't quietly stop visiting and hope they don't notice. Call them, or
tell them at your next visit, and explain what you're doing and why." —
specific, actionable, treats the buyer as a professional.

**On closing a section:**
"It won't feel comfortable. It will feel very clear. That's the point." —
short, direct, doesn't over-explain.

## What the VOD Must Never Do

- Teach beyond what's in the framework. The 90-Day Account Plan is a
  defined methodology. Don't expand it with additional models, borrowed
  frameworks, or tangential ideas that weren't part of the original design.
- Use YouTube structure. No hooks, no pattern interrupts, no "stay with
  me because..." The buyer has already stayed. Just deliver.
- Introduce new characters. Sarah Mitchell is the only worked example.
  Don't create additional fictional buyers or salespeople.
- Change Sarah's numbers mid-programme. Her figures are fixed across all
  four parts for consistency.
- Repeat the same explanatory content across parts. If something was
  explained in Part 1, refer to it briefly in later parts rather than
  re-explaining it.
