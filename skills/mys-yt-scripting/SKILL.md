# MYS YouTube Scripting Skill

YouTube scripts for Mark Young Sales have one job: take a field salesperson
who clicked because the title named their problem, and walk them through a
structured argument that makes them feel understood, makes them see something
differently, and leaves them wanting more. The sale happens later. The video's
job is trust and insight.

---

## Pre-Writing Checklist

Do not start writing until all of the following are confirmed:

- [ ] Title is locked (run mys-title-thumbnail skill if not)
- [ ] Thumbnail text is locked
- [ ] The single core idea is defined — one specific thing the viewer will
      think differently about by the end
- [ ] The target viewer is clear — field salesperson, area manager, or both?
      What do they currently believe? What will the video challenge?
- [ ] Any worked examples needed have been identified (Sarah Mitchell or
      real-territory scenario)

---

## Writing Rules (Apply to Every Section)

These rules are non-negotiable and must be checked before presenting any draft.

**Full sentences at all times.** No fragments used as stylistic devices.
Each sentence does one job.

**Contractions throughout.** "Can't" not "cannot". "Don't" not "do not".
"It's" not "it is". "Haven't" not "have not". "Aren't" not "are not".
"Won't" not "will not". "Didn't" not "did not". The only exception is when
the emphasis specifically requires the full form.

**Conversational but authoritative.** This is a Head of Sales speaking from
experience, not a YouTuber performing for the camera. No hype. No exclamation
points used for energy. No rhetorical filler ("Now, here's the thing...").

**No methodology giveaway.** Free content creates problem-awareness and builds
trust. The 90-Day Account Plan — its specific tools, worksheets, and framework
steps — is paid content. Name what exists. Don't hand it over.

---

## Setup/Payoff Thread (Runs Through the Entire Script)

This is the most important structural principle and must be tracked
consciously throughout every script, not just in the hook.

**The principle:** Every statement that raises a question in the viewer's
mind is a setup. The payoff must come before they lose patience. If you set
something up and don't pay it off, viewers feel cheated and leave. If you
pay off too early (give the answer before the viewer wants it), they feel
nothing and leave.

**The viewer's curiosity should look like a wave, not a flatline.**

- The hook contains the macro setup for the whole video
- Each brick opens with a micro setup (the transition) that raises curiosity
- Each brick closes with a payoff that releases the tension
- The bridge is the final payoff for the macro setup from the hook
- The CTA comes after the final payoff — not before

**Track this actively:** Before finishing a draft, list every setup that
has been created and confirm each one has been paid off before the CTA.
If anything is set up and not paid off, it must be resolved or removed.

## Section 1 — Hook (First 30–60 seconds)

The hook is the highest-stakes section of the script. Its only job is to
pay off the click and earn the next 30 seconds. It doesn't introduce Mark.
It doesn't explain what the video will cover. It grabs.

### Hook Structure

**Line 1 — Mirror the title.**
The very first line should feel like the natural next sentence after the
title. If the title is "Your Pipeline Is Lying to You", the opening line
should land in that same space. "Your pipeline is lying to you" or
"Most of what's in your pipeline right now isn't going to close" — the
viewer clicked because the title named something they felt. Line 1
confirms they were right to click.

**Lines 2–4 — Name the problem specifically.**
Not the general topic — the specific feeling. What does this problem
feel like from the inside? What does the viewer do every week that makes
this problem real? Make them recognise their own situation, not a general
description of a problem category.

**Lines 5–8 — Set up the macro payoff.**
Tell the viewer what they'll have by the end of the video — but without
paying it off yet. Create the question, don't answer it. "By the end of
this, you'll know exactly why that's happening — and what to do about it"
is a setup. "Here's why that's happening: X" is a payoff. The setup
belongs in the hook. The payoff belongs in the body or bridge.

**The hook must not:**
- Introduce Mark by name and credentials before earning the right
- Explain the structure of the video ("In this video, I'm going to...")
- Ask a question the viewer might answer "no" to
- Use hype language that doesn't match the calm MYS delivery style
- Give away the answer before it's been earned

### Hook Length

Target: 150–250 words. Any shorter and it hasn't done the job. Any longer
and the viewer has already left.

## Section 2 — Body (The Bricks)

The body is built from bricks. Each brick makes one point. Together, the
bricks build the argument.

### Brick Structure

Each brick has three parts:

**Transition (Setup):** The sentence that opens the brick. It tells the
viewer what's coming in a way that makes them curious — without paying
anything off. This is the setup for this brick's payoff.

Example of a weak transition: "The next thing I want to talk about is
call management."
Example of a strong transition: "Here's where most of the wasted time
actually lives — and it's not where you'd expect."

**Emotion half (Problem + Example):**
The problem is what the viewer currently experiences or believes.
The example makes it visible — a story, a scenario, or a concrete
illustration that lets them picture it. Sarah Mitchell and her territory
are the primary worked example vehicle for MYS. Use her when a specific
number or situation would help — 170 accounts, £1.85M territory, 43 A
accounts, 34 B accounts, 120 cycle slots per 8-week cycle.

The emotion half speaks to the fast brain. It should feel easy to follow,
not effortful. If it requires concentration to understand, simplify it.

**Logic half (Lesson/Payoff):**
The logical conclusion or reframe. This is the payoff for the brick's
setup. By the end of this half, the viewer should be able to say "I get
it." It doesn't need to be long — sometimes a single clear sentence is
the payoff. Don't over-explain once the point has landed.

### Brick Calibration

Not every point needs a full emotion half. If the point is simple and
immediately obvious, a long example wastes the viewer's time. Ask: does
this point need to be shown before it can be understood, or can it just
be stated and accepted?

If the point is counterintuitive, needs to feel real before the viewer
will believe it, or requires emotional buy-in — the emotion half needs
to be substantial. If the point is logical and self-evident once stated —
keep the emotion half short or skip it.

### Body Length

MYS videos should be substantive enough to feel worth the click, but not
padded. Target 3–5 bricks for most videos. A 5-brick video at proper
depth will run 6–9 minutes. Do not pad to hit an arbitrary length target.

### What the Body Must Not Do

- Give away the 90-Day Account Plan methodology in specific steps
- Use jargon without explanation (CRM, COS, KPIs — fine if explained,
  not fine if assumed)
- Repeat the same point in different words across multiple bricks
- Overload with information — one point per brick, one brick at a time

## Section 3 — Bridge (30–60 seconds)

The bridge is the payoff for the macro setup created in the hook. It lands
the emotional conclusion before asking for anything.

**Its job:** Make the viewer feel the weight of what they've just learned,
and connect it to something bigger than the specific points made in the body.
This is where the "that's what this is really about" moment lives.

**MYS bridge pattern:** The core thesis of the channel — that most sales
problems are territory and account problems, not selling problems — is the
landing point for almost every video. The bridge is where that lands most
cleanly. Not stated didactically ("So what I want you to take from this is...")
but arrived at naturally ("Most people in this situation try to solve it by
calling more often or preparing better. That doesn't fix it, because that's
not where the problem is.")

The bridge must not:
- Introduce new information the viewer hasn't been prepared for
- Start the CTA before the conclusion has landed
- Be skipped — it's the connective tissue between argument and ask

---

## Section 4 — CTA (15–30 seconds)

One CTA per video. Verbal only. One job.

**Standing rule:** Every MYS YouTube video includes a single verbal CTA
with a UTM-tagged link to either the sales page or the lead magnet.
No hard sell. No pressure. No "smash that subscribe button".

**CTA structure:**
- Name what the link is (not just "link in the description")
- One sentence on who it's for and what it does
- That's it

**UTM reminder:** Every CTA link must be UTM-tagged. Confirm which
destination applies for this video (sales page or lead magnet) and
ensure the tracking is in place before the video goes live.

**What the CTA must not do:**
- Reference the £247 Call Package
- Make claims that contradict the calm, measured tone of the video
- Run for more than 30 seconds

## Post-Writing Checklist

Before presenting any draft to Mark:

- [ ] Every sentence is a full sentence — no fragments
- [ ] Contractions are used throughout — check every "cannot", "do not",
      "it is", "have not", "are not", "will not", "did not"
- [ ] Line 1 mirrors the title
- [ ] The hook contains a macro setup that is paid off in the bridge
- [ ] Every brick has a transition (setup), emotion half, and logic half
      (payoff) — or a clear reason why the emotion half was shortened/skipped
- [ ] No brick payoff is given before the viewer has been set up to want it
- [ ] Every setup in the entire script has a corresponding payoff
- [ ] No methodology is given away (specific tools, worksheet steps, the
      Five Filters system in detail)
- [ ] The bridge lands the emotional conclusion before the CTA
- [ ] There is exactly one CTA
- [ ] The UTM-tagged link destination has been confirmed
- [ ] The script sounds like Mark when read aloud — not a YouTuber,
      not a trainer, not a copywriter

Once a script section is approved by Mark, save it to Notion immediately.
