# MYS Title & Thumbnail Skill

Titles and thumbnails are the single biggest lever on a YouTube channel at this
stage. A great video with weak packaging doesn't get clicked. A mediocre video
with strong packaging reaches the wrong audience and kills AVD. The job of this
skill is to produce packaging that is specific to the Mark Young Sales brand,
audience, and positioning — not generic YouTube best practice.

---

## Channel Context

**Channel:** Mark Young Sales  
**Audience:** B2B field salespeople and area managers. They're experienced,
time-poor, and quietly frustrated that their effort isn't producing the results
it should. They're not looking for motivation. They're looking for clarity.  
**Tone:** Calm, measured, authoritative. Head of Sales energy — not YouTube
performance. The packaging must match this.  
**Core thesis:** Most sales problems are account and territory problems. The
channel exists to name that problem and, eventually, provide the solution via
the 90-Day Account Plan.

## Step 1 — Establish the Core Idea

Before writing anything, confirm:

1. **What is the one specific problem this video solves?** Not a topic. A
   specific pain the target viewer feels and hasn't been able to name clearly.
2. **What does the viewer currently believe that this video will challenge or
   confirm?** This is the entry point — the thing they already think, which the
   video will either validate or reframe.
3. **What is the single most surprising or counterintuitive thing the video
   reveals?** This is usually where the strongest title lives.

Do not proceed to title generation until these three questions are answered.

---

## Step 2 — Generate Title Candidates

Generate a minimum of 8 title candidates. Don't filter during generation —
write them out first, evaluate after.

### Title Rules (Non-Negotiable)

- **Under 50 characters.** YouTube truncates longer titles in some contexts.
  Count the characters. Flag anything over 50.
- **No colons.** They make titles harder to read and look AI-generated.
  Use a full stop or em dash if a break is needed.
- **No "I" until credibility is established.** The audience doesn't know
  Mark yet. "I" puts the creator before the viewer's problem.
- **No keyword stuffing.** YouTube reads transcripts and analyses footage.
  Jamming in extra words to rank makes titles worse for humans.
- **Be specific, not clever.** Intrigue is good. Confusion is not.
  A title should feel immediately clear on what problem it's naming.

### BENS Check

Run every title candidate through BENS. You need at least one element strong.

**B — Big:** Does the result or problem feel significant? Would a field
salesperson read this and think "that matters to me"? If it feels small,
push the claim further.

**E — Easy:** Does the path to the result or the fix feel achievable?
This is less critical for MYS titles (the audience respects rigour over
quick-fix promises) but matters if the video is solution-led.

**N — New:** Does this feel like something they haven't heard before, or
a way of seeing something familiar that's genuinely different? The most
powerful element for this audience. They've heard generic sales advice.
They haven't heard it framed through territory and account logic.

**S — Safe:** Would a cold viewer with no knowledge of Mark Young trust
that this video is worth their time? Specificity is the primary trust
signal here — specific numbers, specific claims, specific problems.

### Specific Techniques That Work for This Channel

- **Name the invisible problem:** Something the viewer feels but hasn't
  articulated. "Your pipeline is lying to you" works because they've felt
  the mismatch between what's in the CRM and what's actually going to close.
- **Use specificity as a trust signal:** "60% more business. Half the accounts."
  outperforms any vague version of the same claim.
- **The number device:** A specific number in the title creates instant
  credibility. "Here's the Number" (Video 3) works because it implies there's
  a precise answer, not a guess.
- **The reframe:** Taking something the viewer believes is positive and
  showing it's actually a problem. "The accounts you trust most might be
  the ones costing you most" is a reframe.
- **Mirror the hook:** The best titles for MYS set up the opening line of
  the script. If the title names the problem, the first line of the video
  pays it off immediately. Keep this in mind — the title and hook should
  feel like two halves of the same sentence.

## Step 3 — Generate Thumbnail Text Candidates

Thumbnail text is a compressed title with a different job. It doesn't explain
— it creates a feeling. Generate 5–8 candidates per title shortlist.

### Thumbnail Rules (Non-Negotiable)

- **Dark background, consistent photography.** Locked brand decision.
  Do not suggest light backgrounds or graphic-only thumbnails.
- **1–2 words. 3 words maximum in exceptional cases.**
- **Must not repeat the title's words.** The thumbnail and title work as a
  unit — they should add to each other, not say the same thing twice.
- **Must complete or extend the title's thought.** The combination of
  thumbnail text + title should together make the viewer ask "why?" or
  "how?" or "what?". If you can remove the thumbnail text and the title
  still says everything, the thumbnail text isn't working.
- **Think in emotions and states, not descriptions.** OFF BALANCE, SIGNAL
  TRAP, PROOF — these describe a feeling or a verdict, not a topic.

### Thumbnail Text Approaches That Work

- **The verdict:** What the video concludes in one word. PROOF, BROKEN, FIXED.
- **The feeling:** The emotional state the viewer is in before they know the
  answer. OFF BALANCE, OVERLOAD, STUCK.
- **The trap:** What the video reveals they've been doing wrong. SIGNAL TRAP,
  BLIND SPOT, DEAD WEIGHT.
- **The number withheld:** Show a number with context missing. STOP AT (blurred)
  works because the viewer wants to know what the number is.

---

## Step 4 — Evaluate and Shortlist

After generating candidates, apply this evaluation sequence:

1. **Read the title aloud.** Does it sound like something a person would say,
   or does it sound written? If it sounds written, it needs editing.
2. **Count the characters.** Flag anything over 50. Rewrite to get under if
   possible without losing the core idea.
3. **Check BENS.** Confirm at least one element is strong. If none are, the
   title is too generic.
4. **Check the thumbnail pairing.** Title + thumbnail text together — do they
   create a question in the viewer's mind? If you can read both and feel
   nothing, the pairing isn't working.
5. **Check against the locked five.** Are you accidentally retreading the same
   territory as an existing video? Different angle, different problem, different
   words.

Shortlist to 2–3 title candidates maximum before presenting to Mark.

---

## Step 5 — Present for Decision

Present shortlisted options clearly:

```
TITLE OPTION A — [title] ([character count])
BENS: [which elements are present and why]
THUMBNAIL: [thumbnail text] — [one sentence explaining the pairing logic]

TITLE OPTION B — [title] ([character count])
BENS: [which elements are present and why]
THUMBNAIL: [thumbnail text] — [one sentence explaining the pairing logic]
```

Give a clear recommendation. State which option you think is strongest and
why in one sentence. Don't hedge. Mark has final say.

---

## Step 6 — Lock and Save

Once Mark confirms the title and thumbnail text:

1. State both locked decisions clearly.
2. Save to the relevant Notion page immediately — do not defer.
3. If this is a new video, create a Notion page under the main workflow page
   and record: locked title, locked thumbnail text, character count, BENS
   analysis, and the video idea it came from.

## Repackaging — When to Consider It

Only raise repackaging after a 7-day performance review. Do not suggest
it based on views alone.

**Repackage may be warranted if:**
- CTR is below 3% AND Browse Features % is low (algorithm not pushing it)
- Both views AND AVD are low (packaging and content both failing)

**Do not repackage if:**
- Views are low but AVD is strong — this is a packaging problem, not a
  content problem. Address the title and thumbnail.
- AVD is low but views are okay — this is a content/structure problem.
  Repackaging won't fix it.
- It's been less than 7 days — insufficient data.

**Repackaging process:**
Run the new title through the full Step 1–5 process above. Don't rush
a repackage. A bad second title is worse than leaving the first one.

---

## Standing Rules

- Never suggest adding a colon to a title.
- Never suggest a thumbnail with a light background.
- Never use "I" in a title for the MYS channel at this stage.
- Never present more than 3 title options at a time — more choices don't
  help, they delay the decision.
- Always give a clear recommendation — don't present options without a view.
- The £247 Call Package is never mentioned in any public-facing copy. Do not
  reference it in titles, thumbnails, or descriptions.
- Dave Jeltema's feedback principle: titles should feel like they're speaking
  directly to one specific person's problem, not broadcasting to a crowd.
