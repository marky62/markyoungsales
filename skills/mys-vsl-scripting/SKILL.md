# MYS VSL Scripting Skill

A VSL is not a YouTube video and it's not a VOD module. Its job is singular:
inspire the right person to take one specific action. It does this by
identifying the false beliefs that are stopping them from buying, and
dismantling those beliefs through story and evidence — not by teaching the
methodology. If the VSL teaches, it sounds hard and viewers leave. If it
inspires and removes objections, it converts.

## The Domino Statement

This is the North Star for every VSL written for Mark Young Sales.
Every story, every section, every line of the script must reinforce it.

**"If I can convince a B2B field salesperson that their results are a direct
consequence of how they manage their accounts and territory — not their
effort, skill, or market conditions — then all other objections are
irrelevant."**

If a proposed section or story doesn't reinforce this, it doesn't belong
in the VSL.

---

## The Three False Beliefs

These are the beliefs that stop the right buyer from purchasing. The VSL
exists to dismantle all three.

**False Belief 1 — External ("It's the market / territory")**
"My results are down to conditions I can't control. The market is difficult.
My patch is tough. There isn't much more I can do."

**False Belief 2 — Effort ("I'm already working as hard as I can")**
"I'm putting the hours in. Full diary, hitting my activity targets. If
working harder hasn't fixed it, nothing will."

**False Belief 3 — Vehicle ("Account planning sounds complicated")**
"I've tried planning before. It's a spreadsheet you update in January
and ignore until the next review. I don't have time for something that
won't make a real difference."

Every section of the VSL is either building toward dismantling one of
these, or it's between sections and shouldn't be there.

## The Locked 12-Step Structure

The VSL follows the Ed Lawrence 12-step framework, adapted for Mark Young
Sales. The locked V4 script demonstrates this in practice. The structure
is fixed — sections can be revised but not removed or reordered without
a strong reason.

### Step 1 — Hook
Stops the right viewer and filters out the wrong one. The buyer is a B2B
field salesperson whose results aren't matching their effort. The hook
names that state precisely. It does not make a big promise — it makes a
true observation the viewer immediately recognises.

**Current locked hook:**
"If you're a B2B field salesperson and your results aren't reflecting the
effort you're putting in — this is worth a few minutes of your time. You
already know something isn't working, that this job shouldn't feel as hard
as it does at the moment. I'm going to show you something that explains
why it feels that way, and what you can do about fixing it."

**What the hook must do:**
- Qualify the viewer in the first sentence
- Name the felt experience, not the abstract problem
- Create enough curiosity to earn the next 60 seconds
- Sound like Mark, not like a copywriter

### Step 2 — Set Up (The Three Secrets Preview)
Tells the viewer what they're going to learn. Specific enough to create
curiosity. Vague enough not to give it away. Sets up all three false
beliefs without naming them as false beliefs.

**Current locked set up:**
"I'm going to cover three things. Why effort may not be the problem.
Why working harder on some of your accounts won't just fail to help —
it'll actively make things worse. And what a proper territory framework
actually does, based on a real case. Stay with me."

### Step 3 — Ethos (Credibility)
30–60 seconds. Establishes why the viewer should believe what follows.
This is not the place for modesty. Specific experience, real results,
named roles.

Mark's credibility is: thirty years in B2B field sales. Area Manager.
Sales team leader. Head of Sales. Territory built from scratch. Teams
taken on when not hitting numbers. The 60% more revenue / half the
accounts case study is the anchor.

Don't add to or alter the credibility claims without Mark's confirmation.

### Step 4 — Secret 1 (Dismantles False Belief 1 — External)
Story: The team that was blaming the market. They were experienced,
full diaries, genuine effort. Once we stopped looking at activity and
started looking at the accounts, the picture changed.

The story must start at the catalyst — not at the backstory. The discovery
("the market wasn't the problem") is the pivot. Don't resolve it fully here —
tease the resolution to pull through to Secret 2.

### Step 5 — Secret 2 (Dismantles False Belief 2 — Effort)
The insight that doing more in the wrong place doesn't move numbers.
Names the specific trap: activity produces the appearance of progress
without producing results. Good salespeople can spend years in this
cycle because nothing about it feels wrong from the inside.

Does not resolve with the answer yet. Teases that there's something
specific going on underneath the surface.

### Step 6 — Secret 3 (Dismantles False Belief 3 — Vehicle)
The case study resolution. The account base was cut from 750 to roughly
half. The result: 60% more business over the following period. The
explanation: not because they became better salespeople, but because
they stopped spreading time across accounts that couldn't justify it.

**This is the most important secret.** It's the proof that the vehicle
(the methodology) works. It needs to be the longest and most detailed
of the three. The numbers must be accurate: 750 accounts, roughly half,
60% more business.

### Step 7 — The Offer
Introduces the 90-Day Account Plan by name. States what it is (four-part
on-demand programme), how it works (buyer's own numbers, own territory,
own pace), and what's included (six working templates — named specifically).
States the price: £397.

Mentions The Mentorship Programme waitlist in one line only. No more.

### Step 8 — Final Choice
Two paths. No pressure, no false urgency. Just the honest choice:
carry on as you are, or spend time going through this properly.

**The Final Choice must not:**
- Create artificial scarcity
- Use high-pressure language
- Promise transformation — it promises clarity and a framework

## Writing Rules (Non-Negotiable)

**Full sentences at all times.** No fragments as stylistic devices.

**Contractions throughout.** "Can't" not "cannot". "Don't" not "do not".
"It's" not "it is". "Haven't" not "have not". "Aren't" not "are not".
"Won't" not "will not". "Didn't" not "did not".

**No teaching in the VSL.** The moment the script starts explaining how
the methodology works in detail, it has crossed from inspiration to
instruction. That's the VOD's job. The VSL names the problem, tells
the story of the result, and invites the viewer to get the method.
It does not deliver the method.

**No hyperbole.** The case study numbers (60% more revenue, account base
halved from 750) are real and powerful. They don't need embellishment.
Every claim in the VSL must be one Mark can stand behind.

**Mark's voice, not a copywriter's voice.** Read every line aloud.
If it sounds like it was written by someone who doesn't work in B2B
field sales, rewrite it until it doesn't.

## The "Ali's Bins" Principle

This principle is locked for MYS copy. It applies directly to VSL writing.

Secrets 1 and 2 build intrigue and land the problem — they do not explain
the solution or name the methodology. Secret 3 delivers the result (the
case study) but not the process. The process is what the buyer pays for.

If at any point a VSL draft starts explaining how the framework works —
the specific classification system, the Five Filters, the Account Sales
Plan structure — it has broken this principle. Stop. Pull back to the
result and cut the explanation.

## New VSL Versions

If a new VSL is needed (new offer, new angle, updated case study):

1. Start from the domino statement — confirm it still applies or rewrite it
2. Confirm the three false beliefs for this specific offer
3. Identify the stories — Mark's own story first, client stories second
4. Write the set-up bullets before writing any story sections
5. Label the new version clearly (V5, V6, etc.)
6. Save to Notion as a new page — do not overwrite the current locked version
7. Flag which sections are new vs carried from the previous version
